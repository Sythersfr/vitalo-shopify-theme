A story you might recognize

“I was 58 when I stopped
trusting my own brain.”

I used to be the sharpest person in the room. Not in a flashy way — I just knew things. Names. Numbers. Where I put my keys, what I needed at the store, what I was about to say mid-sentence.

Then somewhere around 55, things started to slip. Not dramatically. Just … quietly. I'd lose a word. Forget a name I'd known for 20 years. Read a page and realize I absorbed nothing. I started writing everything down — not because I wanted to, but because I didn't trust myself not to.

By 58, the fatigue was constant. Not sleepy-tired — deep tired. Like my brain was running through mud. My joints ached every morning. I stopped volunteering for things. Stopped saying yes to dinners. Started making excuses.

“The worst part wasn't forgetting things. It was not recognizing the person I was becoming.”

I went to my doctor. She ran bloodwork. Everything came back “normal.” She told me this is what happens. That I should get more sleep. Exercise more. Maybe try a crossword puzzle.

I sat in the parking lot afterward and thought: If everything's normal, why do I feel like I'm disappearing?

I tried supplements on my own. Multivitamins. Fish oil. A B12 I saw on TV. Turmeric capsules from the pharmacy. Some weeks I thought maybe they were helping. Most weeks I couldn't tell. Nothing stuck. Nothing brought me back to who I was.

I had nearly accepted it. Nearly decided this was just my life now — smaller, quieter, slower.

But then I learned something that changed everything. Not a miracle. Not a hack. Just a simple truth about what's actually happening inside the body after 50 — and why almost nothing on the market is designed to fix it.

This isn't about willpower. It's about biology.

And once you see what's really going on, you can't unsee it.
